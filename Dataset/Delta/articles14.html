The Delta Variant Shouldn’t Be a Problem for Oil. Here’s Why, Says Goldman Sachs.

By
Barbara Kollmeyer
July 20, 2021 10:53 am ET
  • Order Reprints
  • Print Article
Text size

Oil prices were clawing back some lost ground on Tuesday, after a meltdown driven by an Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries agreement to restore production and worries over the highly contagious Delta variant of the coronavirus that causes Covid-19.

U.S. oil prices on Monday saw the biggest one-day loss since September, with the latter of that one-two punch for oil tied to concerns that rising coronavirus cases could weigh on economic reopenings and hence, demand for the commodity.

But calling it “another manageable speed bump,” a team of strategists at Goldman Sachs, led by Damien Courvalin, brushed aside fears of the variant, saying vaccines should help to limit the hospitalizations that had surged in previous waves, and oil is already facing supply woes.

Granted, much remains unknown about the Delta variant that has been spreading infections like wildfire, chiefly among the unvaccinated, the strategists added.

But they calculated that Tuesday’s 7.5% drop for U.S. crude was equivalent to lowering second-half demand expectations by a “significant 2 million barrels a day, with no offsetting response from the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries and its allies as well as 1 mb/d lower medium-term demand.”

Their bottom-up estimate of the impact from a Delta-fueled wave of infections on global demand—based on Europe’s second wave and a recent one in India—points to a potential 1 mb/d hit for just a couple of months. That is even less if vaccines prove effective at lowering hospitalizations in developed markets, the source of most summer demand improvements.

The team pointed out that the U.K.’s Scientific Advisory Group for Emergencies Covid task force has estimated that hospitalizations—the new criteria according to Goldman—will peak at one-third of the winter wave, with fewer severe cases and shorter hospital stays on average. “If this proves to be the case—with the U.K. the case study for all developed markets—then Monday’s move has overshot,” said the Goldman strategists.

Should the Delta variant point to weakened vaccine protection, the demand hit to crude would last longer, though wouldn’t be much larger given the understanding around the illness now, such as knowledge that masks can reduce transmission.

“Even in this worst-case scenario outcome, the demand hit would still be much smaller than priced by the market on Monday, especially as current prices would likely lead to lower OPEC+ production and—to a lesser extent—shale,” said the Goldman strategists.

In the wake of the decision by the cartel and its allies to boost production by about 400,000 barrels a day a month until levels are fully restored next year, the strategists had already dismissed concerns about a flush of supply. Crude had dropped in the wake of that decision on Monday.

The production quotas for August to December 2021 were 0.5 mb/d below their own expectations and “represent a bigger cumulative loss of supply by year-end than the 1 bm/d Delta demand hit for two months,” they said. Included in that forecast is the view by Goldman that non-OPEC+ North American production will surprise to the downside, and Iranian exports will ramp up later than expected due to an impasse in nuclear talks with the U.S.
